# Portfolio Analysis and Repositioning: Complete Overview

*Prepared as a comprehensive reference for a Traditional IRA repositioning analysis, May 2026*

---

## 1. Executive Summary

This document consolidates a multi-session analysis of a Traditional IRA portfolio, the process of stress-testing it against multiple macro scenarios, and the development of a portfolio scenario simulator to support the decision-making process.

**The starting position:** A six-holding IRA constructed on a defensive thesis that assumed persistent US inflation, consumer weakness, and dollar depreciation. Holdings are XLP (Consumer Staples Select Sector SPDR ETF) 25%, VEA (Vanguard FTSE Developed Markets ETF) 23%, VNQ (Vanguard Real Estate ETF) 20%, VWO (Vanguard FTSE Emerging Markets ETF) 15%, GLDM (SPDR Gold MiniShares Trust) 15%, and VWOB (Vanguard Emerging Markets Government Bond ETF) 2%.

**The core finding:** The current portfolio is defensively miscalibrated. It expresses a bearish macro view through instruments that don't actually deliver the intended protection. XLP has been squeezed by stagflation-lite conditions rather than protected by them. VNQ has behaved like rate-sensitive duration rather than an inflation hedge. The 38% combined international allocation (VEA + VWO) worked as a dollar hedge (Europe returned strongly in 2025 with the dollar decline) but is cyclical equity rather than defensive positioning. The 2% VWOB allocation is too small to matter and behaves like a risk asset anyway.

**The recommendation:** Shift to a "Consensus core" allocation of VEA 22%, GLDM 18%, SCHP (Schwab US TIPS ETF) 18%, SCHD (Schwab US Dividend Equity ETF) 18%, VWO 10%, BND (Vanguard Total Bond Market ETF) 8%, and GOOGL (Alphabet) 6%. This maintains the underlying defensive/inflation-hedge thesis but expresses it through instruments that actually deliver on that role. It adds real bond ballast, a proper inflation hedge in TIPS, and modest US equity participation.

**For short-term money:** A separate "Nest egg" account of VGSH (Vanguard Short-Term Treasury ETF) 60%, SCHD 25%, BND 15% for 6-18 month funds that need capital preservation with modest upside beyond pure Treasury yields.

**Important caveat:** The analytical methodology has known structural flaws documented in Section 8. The specific numerical comparisons between proposed portfolios are less reliable than they appear. The conclusion that the current portfolio is poorly structured is robust; the exact relative ranking of alternatives is not.

### 2.2 Original Thesis

The allocation was constructed to express a specific macro view: US inflation would remain elevated, US consumer demand would weaken, the dollar would decline against major currencies, and US equity markets (particularly tech) would correct.

Each holding was chosen to express this view:

- **XLP** as defensive US equity (consumer staples typically hold up when consumer discretionary weakens)
- **VEA** as a dollar hedge (unhedged international equity rises in USD terms when the dollar falls)
- **VNQ** as an inflation hedge (real assets should reprice with inflation)
- **VWO** for emerging market growth exposure decorrelated from US
- **GLDM** as a currency debasement and tail-risk hedge
- **VWOB** for high-yield fixed income with limited US rate exposure

### 2.3 What Worked

The dollar hedge thesis played out. The US Dollar Index declined roughly 9% during 2025. VEA captured both the underlying equity gains and the currency tailwind, ending the year up substantially. VGK (Vanguard FTSE Europe ETF), a purer Europe proxy, returned 35.44% in 2025, its best year in the past decade.

GLDM also worked structurally. Gold hit an all-time high of $5,589 per ounce on January 28, 2026, driven by central bank buying, dollar weakness, and rate cut expectations.

### 2.4 What Didn't

XLP has been a drag. Consumer staples got squeezed from both directions: input costs elevated by persistent inflation, and consumers trading down to private label offerings. TTM return around +5.7%, well below broad market benchmarks.

VNQ has been rate-sensitive rather than inflation-protective. US commercial real estate has structural issues (office vacancy, refinancing wall) that made REITs a noisy vehicle for the inflation hedge thesis. TTM return around +6.8%.

VWOB at 2% is a token allocation that doesn't meaningfully affect portfolio behavior. It also behaves more like a risk asset (EM credit) than a safe haven.

### 2.5 Overall Performance

Weighted TTM return for the portfolio: approximately +22%. That trailed VOO (Vanguard S&P 500 ETF) at +29% and VTI (Vanguard Total Stock Market ETF) at +26% by 4-7 percentage points, despite carrying substantially less equity risk than either. The dollar hedge and gold allocations did most of the work; the defensive equity and REIT positions were drags.

## 3. Macro Backdrop and Consensus Views

Analysis conducted in mid-May 2026, against a specific macro backdrop that must be understood to interpret the recommendations.

### 3.1 Iran War and Oil Shock

An armed conflict between the US and Iran began in late February 2026, triggering the largest oil supply disruption in history. This has materially shifted macro forecasts across institutions. Oil prices remained elevated through Q2 2026, feeding through to headline CPI and complicating Fed policy decisions.

### 3.2 Recession Probability

Institutional forecasters converged on elevated near-term recession odds:

- **Goldman Sachs**: Raised 12-month recession probability from 20% to 30% due to oil/Iran factors, subsequently revised higher
- **J.P. Morgan**: 35% probability of US and global recession in 2026
- **Moody's/Mark Zandi**: 49% recession probability, potentially crossing 50%
- **EY-Parthenon**: 40% recession probability

Institutional recession probability consensus is roughly 30-45%, significantly above the historical unconditional base rate of about 15% per year.

### 3.3 K-Shaped US Consumer

The consumer picture is not uniformly weak. TD Economics documented a K-shaped pattern where top-quintile households (holding roughly 72% of household wealth) continue spending while bottom-quintile households are squeezed by inflation, rising energy costs, and reduced government transfers. Goldman revised its 2026 discretionary cash flow growth forecast from 5.1% to 3.7%, with the bottom quintile forecast at just 0.8% growth.

### 3.4 Inflation and Stagflation Risk

- OECD forecast: US inflation at 4.2% in 2026 (revised up 1.2 percentage points from prior)
- IMF reference scenario: 3.1% global growth, 4.4% inflation
- IMF adverse scenario: 2.5% growth, 5.4% inflation
- IMF severe scenario: 2.0% growth, inflation exceeding 6%

Two of three IMF scenarios meet stagflation criteria. Dallas Fed research documents the mechanical impact of oil supply shocks on inflation persistence.

### 3.5 AI Bubble Probability

The market for "AI bubble bursts by December 31, 2026" on Polymarket has traded between 17% and 25% through 2026 so far. J.P. Morgan Asset Management explicitly rejects the bubble thesis and remains overweight US equities. Fidelity notes that AI capex is funded almost entirely from earnings rather than debt, differentiating current valuations from the dot-com era. Wellington advises allocators to revisit defensive equity strategies while acknowledging that defensives may lag tech for extended periods.

### 3.6 Gold Outlook

Despite a 16% correction from January highs, institutional gold forecasts remained bullish:

- J.P. Morgan: Q4 2026 average forecast of $5,055/oz
- Wells Fargo, Goldman: Maintained bullish targets
- World Gold Council base case: +5% to +15% for 2026; recession scenario +15% to +30%; strong-dollar scenario -5% to -20%

Structural drivers (central bank buying, dollar debasement, rate cuts) remained intact.

### 3.7 Synthesis

The consensus picture in May 2026 was materially more bearish than 6-12 months earlier. Recession probability of 30-45% is roughly 2-3x historical base rates. Stagflation is a live concern rather than a tail risk. The AI bubble question is unresolved but not the base case. This backdrop shaped both the probability weights in our simulator defaults and the recommendation to reposition rather than pivot fully bullish.

## 4. Portfolio Analysis Framework

We developed a scenario-based framework to test portfolios across multiple macro outcomes rather than optimizing for a single expected outcome.

### 4.1 The Seven Scenarios

| ID | Scenario | Description | Default Probability |
|---|---|---|---|
| `ai` | AI productivity boom | AI delivers real productivity gains, broad rally, tech leads, inflation cools | 8% |
| `refl` | Reflation / re-acceleration | Growth picks up, moderate inflation, weak dollar, international and value win | 7% |
| `sl` | Soft landing | Inflation cools, moderate equity gains, bonds stable | 10% |
| `sq` | Status quo | Tech-led growth continues, mild inflation, no recession | 20% |
| `tb` | Tech bubble bursts | Nasdaq -30%, broad market hit, defensives hold up | 15% |
| `rec` | Broad recession | US growth contracts, all equity drops, Fed cuts aggressively | 22% |
| `stag` | Stagflation | Inflation persists, growth weak, TIPS and gold shine | 18% |

Bearish scenarios total 55%, bullish/neutral 45%. This reflects the consensus outlook described in Section 3.

### 4.2 Return Assumptions

Each ETF and single stock has an expected return under each scenario. These are point estimates based on historical patterns and current conditions. Full assumption table is in Appendix A.

Illustrative example for VOO (Vanguard S&P 500 ETF):

- AI boom: +25%
- Reflation: +15%
- Soft landing: +13%
- Status quo: +14%
- Tech burst: -12%
- Recession: -15%
- Stagflation: -4%

### 4.3 Calculations

**Portfolio return in a scenario:**
```
R_scenario(P) = Σ(w_i × r_i,scenario)
```
Where w_i is the weight of ticker i in portfolio P and r_i,scenario is that ticker's assumed return in the scenario.

**Weighted expected 1-year return:**
```
E[R_1Y] = Σ(prob_scenario × R_scenario(P))
```

**Multi-year projection:**
Monte Carlo simulation of 500 paths, each 10 years long. In each path year, a scenario is sampled from the probability distribution and its return applied to compound the portfolio value. Percentiles (P10, P50, P90) are computed across paths for each year.

### 4.4 Asset Categories

Holdings are grouped into eight categories for organizational clarity:

- US equity: Broad market & dividend
- US equity: Growth & tech
- US equity: Sector & factor
- International equity
- Individual stocks
- Fixed income: Core & long duration
- Fixed income: Cash & short duration
- Real assets & commodities

## 7. Recommendations

### 7.1 Primary IRA Recommendation: Shift to Consensus Core

Move from the current allocation to Consensus Core:

**Sell:**
- XLP (Consumer Staples Select Sector SPDR ETF) 25% → 0%
- VNQ (Vanguard Real Estate ETF) 20% → 0%
- VWOB (Vanguard Emerging Markets Government Bond ETF) 2% → 0%
- VEA (Vanguard FTSE Developed Markets ETF) 23% → 22% (trim slightly)
- VWO (Vanguard FTSE Emerging Markets ETF) 15% → 10% (trim)
- GLDM (SPDR Gold MiniShares Trust) 15% → 18% (bump up)

**Buy:**
- SCHD (Schwab US Dividend Equity ETF) 0% → 18%
- SCHP (Schwab US TIPS ETF) 0% → 18%
- BND (Vanguard Total Bond Market ETF) 0% → 8%
- GOOGL (Alphabet) 0% → 6%

### 7.2 Rationale

**Removing XLP:** Consumer staples don't perform the intended defensive role in stagflation-lite conditions. TIPS + short-duration Treasuries + dividend equity express the defensive thesis better.

**Removing VNQ:** REITs behave more like rate-sensitive duration than inflation-hedged real assets. Gold does the inflation-hedge job more cleanly.

**Removing VWOB:** 2% is too small to matter. Real bond ballast comes from BND and SCHP.

**Trimming VEA and VWO:** Still meaningful international exposure (32% combined) for dollar hedge, but not concentrated.

**Adding SCHD:** US equity exposure that isn't tech-heavy. Dividend/quality screen aligns with defensive thesis.

**Adding SCHP:** Direct stagflation hedge. TIPS principal adjusts with CPI.

**Adding BND:** Recession ballast. Investment-grade US bonds provide real diversification when equity falls.

**Adding GOOGL:** Specific name conviction play. Alphabet's fundamentals differentiate it from generic tech exposure.

**Bumping GLDM:** Gold is doing real work in the portfolio and consensus targets remain bullish.

### 7.3 IRA Tax Advantages

Executing this reposition in a Traditional IRA is tax-free. No capital gains realized, no wash-sale rules to navigate, no tax reporting on the trades. This is a substantial advantage over doing similar restructuring in a taxable brokerage account.

### 7.4 Execution Notes

Consider staging the trades over 30-60 days to reduce risk of executing all trades at unfavorable moments. This is less critical in an IRA than a taxable account, but psychologically helpful.

Alternatively, execute all trades in a single session to minimize tracking error against the intended new allocation.

### 7.5 Nest Egg Recommendation

For funds that might be needed in 6-18 months but where pure capital preservation feels too limiting:

**VGSH 60%, SCHD 25%, BND 15%**

Held in a separate account from the IRA. This is not a good use of IRA tax-advantaged space for growth (short-duration Treasuries and modest equity yield don't benefit as much from tax deferral). Better held in a taxable account or a high-yield savings account structure.

If the risk tolerance is truly low, simplify to 100% VGSH for pure Treasury yield with modest duration.

### 7.6 What NOT to Do

**Don't chase 2025 performance.** The Optimistic portfolio backtests better in bullish scenarios, but committing there requires believing the tech rally continues at recent pace. This bet may pay off but has been the wrong bet for defensive investors historically.

**Don't over-optimize.** The difference between Consensus Core and Conservative is within the noise of model assumptions. Pick one and hold it. Constant repositioning based on updated model runs would be worse than either choice.

**Don't add more tickers.** 7 holdings is at the upper limit of what most investors can manage. Adding QQQ, VUG, VIG, TLT, or others would create overlap without diversification benefit.

## 8. Critical Assessment of the Methodology

The analytical framework has real strengths but also real flaws that limit how much confidence to place in specific numerical results. This section documents them honestly.

### 8.1 Independent Annual Sampling

The Monte Carlo simulation treats each year as an independent draw from the scenario distribution. Real markets have persistence (bull markets cluster, recessions run multi-year cycles) and mean reversion (bad years followed by recovery years). Independent sampling can produce three consecutive recession years, which is far more pessimistic than history suggests. It can also produce runs of AI boom scenarios that are unrealistically bullish.

**Impact:** Tail outcomes (P10 and P90) are overstated in both directions. Median outcomes are approximately correct.

### 8.2 Near-Term Probabilities Applied Long-Term

Consensus recession probabilities (30-45%) reflect institutional views of the next 12 months. We apply the same probability to year 6 of the simulation, which has no reason to inherit current conditions. Historical unconditional recession probability is closer to 15% per year.

**Impact:** 10-year projections systematically understate equity returns.

### 8.3 Scenario Returns Anchored to Recent Memory

Return assumptions were calibrated to recent severe cases (2008, 2022) rather than historical averages. Average recession-year S&P 500 return is -10% to -12%, not the -15% assumed. Some recessions (1980, 1990, 2020) were actually positive equity years.

**Impact:** Bearish scenarios are too punishing. This depresses all equity portfolios in the comparison.

### 8.4 Scenarios Not Mutually Exclusive

Tech bubble bursts and broad recession can occur together (2000-2002, 2008). Stagflation can include a recession (1974). The framework treats these as separate independent draws but historically they're correlated. Combined bearish weight of 55% partially double-counts overlapping bad outcomes.

**Impact:** Bearish weight is inflated relative to true probability of "some kind of bad year."

### 8.5 Point Estimates Without Variance

Every scenario return is a single number. "Recession = VTI -15%." But recession years vary widely (1990: VOO -3%, 2008: VOO -37%). Collapsing this to a single point loses within-scenario variance.

**Impact:** P10 to P90 spread is too narrow. The framework understates how much luck matters.

### 8.6 Ticker-Level Inconsistencies

Some assumptions don't form a coherent system:

- GOOGL at +22% in status quo vs +45% in AI boom implies "status quo" is meaningfully worse for Google than recent history, despite the description saying "tech-led growth continues"
- VGT at +24% in status quo but +18% in soft landing has the ordering wrong (soft landing should be more bullish for equity)
- XLP at -3% in recession is too benign; XLP was -15% in 2008

**Impact:** Cross-portfolio comparisons are less reliable than they appear because ticker assumptions don't cohere.

### 8.7 No Correlation Modeling

GLDM and SCHP both supposedly hedge inflation but correlate ~0.4. VEA and VWO correlate ~0.7. Holding both large in each pair provides less diversification than the framework implies. The portfolios look more diversified than they are.

**Impact:** Actual portfolio variance is higher than the framework predicts.

### 8.8 No Fee Compounding

Weighted ER shown as a static number. In reality, a 0.25% expense ratio difference compounds to roughly 6% lost wealth over 30 years. The multi-year projections don't reflect this.

**Impact:** Higher-fee portfolios (like those including AVUV at 0.25%) look more attractive than they are over long horizons.

### 8.9 No Passive Benchmark

The honest question for any IRA allocation is: "Would VT 100% (Vanguard Total World Stock ETF) or Target-Date 2055 do better with less complexity?" We never included that as a comparison. The custom allocations may or may not beat the passive benchmark; the framework can't tell us.

**Impact:** The framework may be optimizing at the wrong level of decision-making.

### 8.10 Framing Biases

**"Consensus" is a halo.** Calling default probabilities "consensus-aligned" gives them undeserved credibility. Institutional forecasters are systematically biased toward predicting bad outcomes (asymmetric reputational risk).

**Sliding probabilities feels like science but is belief expression.** The interactivity implies analytical rigor. In practice, it's a weighting exercise where your belief drives your result.

**Clean UI implies confidence.** Two-decimal precision on weighted expected returns conveys precision that doesn't exist in the underlying assumptions.

### 8.11 What Is Still Reliable

Despite these flaws, some conclusions are robust:

- The current portfolio (XLP-heavy, VNQ-heavy) is poorly structured for any plausible scenario set
- The direction of the recommended changes (add TIPS, add bonds, reduce sector concentration, add some US equity) is correct
- The nest egg allocation logic (VGSH base, modest SCHD tilt) is sound

The specific relative ranking of the four IRA-style portfolios is within the noise of the model assumptions. Any of Conservative, Consensus Core, or Optimistic is defensible depending on personal conviction. The framework can't strongly distinguish between them.

## 9. Simpler Alternative Framework

Given the methodology flaws, a simpler decision framework is worth having.

### 9.1 Equity vs Bond Allocation

The single most important decision. Historical guide is "100 minus your age" for equity allocation, though many practitioners now use "110 minus age" or "120 minus age" given longer lifespans. For a mid-career person 20-30 years from retirement:

- 70-80% equity, 20-30% bonds is a defensible standard
- 60-70% equity, 30-40% bonds if more risk-averse
- 80-90% equity, 10-20% bonds if more aggressive

This single decision dominates all others.

### 9.2 US vs International Split

Within equity:

- 60-70% US, 30-40% international is the standard institutional recommendation
- Adjust toward international if you have a dollar-weakness view
- Adjust toward US if you have an AI/tech productivity view
- Historical performance since 1990 shows roughly equal returns with very different timing

### 9.3 Alternatives Sleeve

Small allocation (5-15% of total portfolio) to real assets:

- Gold for inflation and tail hedge
- REITs for real estate exposure
- Optional and can be zero without dramatic impact

### 9.4 Single Stock Cap

Individual stocks should be capped at 5-10% of total portfolio, regardless of conviction. Single-stock risk is real (companies fail, sectors change, management makes mistakes).

### 9.5 The Target-Date Fund Reality Check

Vanguard Target Retirement 2055 has:

- Expense ratio: 0.08%
- Automatic rebalancing
- Automatic glide path (more conservative over time)
- Diversified across US equity, international equity, bonds, TIPS
- Long-term historical performance competitive with most custom allocations

Custom allocation only makes sense if you have specific views the target-date fund doesn't express (like "I want more gold" or "I want no single-stock risk"). Otherwise the complexity isn't worth it.

The Consensus Core portfolio we recommended expresses specific views (dollar hedge via VEA, stagflation hedge via SCHP, tech conviction via GOOGL) that the target-date fund doesn't. Whether those views are worth the complexity is your judgment call.

### 9.6 What This Looks Like in Practice

If you accept the simpler framework:

- 70% equity: 45% VTI + 25% VXUS (broad US + broad international)
- 20% bonds: 15% BND + 5% SCHP (core bonds + inflation protection)
- 10% alternatives: 10% GLDM (gold)

Four holdings. Total expense ratio around 0.04%. Historically competitive with almost any 7-holding custom allocation.

If you want to keep the dollar hedge and single-stock conviction:

- 60% equity: 30% VTI + 20% VEA + 10% VWO
- 20% bonds: 12% BND + 8% SCHP
- 10% alternatives: 10% GLDM
- 5% single stock: 5% GOOGL
- 5% cash: 5% VGSH

Seven holdings. Similar to Consensus Core but with slightly different weights. This is essentially the recommended portfolio, restated.

The point is: the specific 7-holding portfolio matters less than the underlying structure of "meaningful international, real bond ballast, small inflation hedge, small conviction position." Any allocation with that structure will do approximately similarly.

## 10. Portfolio Simulator Technical Specification

Complete specification of the simulator functionality, sufficient to rebuild the tool from scratch.

### 10.1 High-Level Architecture

A single-page React application with:

- **State:** Scenario probabilities, ticker assumptions, portfolio compositions
- **Persistence:** Browser storage API (window.storage) for saving state between sessions
- **Calculations:** Portfolio returns per scenario, weighted expected returns, Monte Carlo compound projections
- **UI:** Composition table, scenario sliders, expected return chart, performance matrix, editable assumptions, editable portfolios

Built with React (hooks), Recharts for charting, Lucide React for icons, and Tailwind CSS for styling.

### 10.3 Core Calculations

**Portfolio return in scenario:**
```javascript
function portfolioReturn(portfolio, scenarioId, tickers) {
  let total = 0;
  for (const [ticker, weight] of Object.entries(portfolio.weights)) {
    if (tickers[ticker] && typeof tickers[ticker][scenarioId] === 'number') {
      total += (weight / 100) * tickers[ticker][scenarioId];
    }
  }
  return total;
}
```

**Weighted expected 1-year return:**
```javascript
function weightedExpected(portfolio, probs, tickers) {
  const probSum = SCENARIOS.reduce((s, sc) => s + probs[sc.id], 0);
  if (probSum === 0) return 0;
  return SCENARIOS.reduce(
    (sum, sc) => sum + (probs[sc.id] / probSum) * portfolioReturn(portfolio, sc.id, tickers),
    0
  );
}
```

**Portfolio weighted expense ratio:**
```javascript
function portfolioWeightedER(portfolio, tickers) {
  let total = 0, weight = 0;
  for (const [t, w] of Object.entries(portfolio.weights)) {
    if (tickers[t] && typeof tickers[t].er === 'number') {
      total += (w / 100) * tickers[t].er;
      weight += w / 100;
    }
  }
  return weight > 0 ? total / weight : 0;
}
```

**Monte Carlo projection:**

500 runs of 10 years each. In each year of each run, sample a scenario weighted by probabilities, apply that scenario's return, compound.

### 10.5 Interactivity Patterns

**Slider auto-balance:**
When user changes slider N to new value V:
1. Compute delta = V - old value
2. Sum weights of other sliders
3. Reduce other sliders proportionally by delta (or increase, if delta negative)
4. Ensure final sum equals 100 exactly (adjust largest other slider for rounding)

**Live updates:**
All calculations use React useMemo hooks. When probabilities change, weighted returns recalculate. When portfolios change, all dependent charts update. When ticker assumptions change, everything recalculates.

**Monte Carlo timing:**
Monte Carlo runs on every dependency change (probabilities, portfolios, tickers). 500 runs of 10 years across 5 portfolios = 25,000 sampling operations, executes in under 100ms on typical hardware.

### 10.6 Persistence

Use window.storage API (if available) to persist:
- Scenario probabilities: key "probs"
- Ticker assumptions: key "tickers"
- Portfolio compositions: key "portfolios"

**Load pattern:**
On mount, attempt to load each key. If found, merge with current defaults so that new scenarios or tickers added in code get sensible values rather than undefined.

**Save pattern:**
useEffect on each state slice that saves to storage on change, but only after initial load has completed (avoids overwriting stored data with defaults during load).

**Reset button:**
Deletes all three storage keys and resets state to defaults.

### 10.7 Order Sensitivity

React hooks in the main component must be declared in dependency order:

1. State declarations (useState)
2. Persistence effects (useEffect for load/save)
3. Handler functions
4. Monte Carlo useMemo (no dependencies on other memos)
5. chartData useMemo (depends on monteCarlo)
6. allTickers useMemo
7. groupedTickers useMemo
8. bestPerScenario useMemo

Getting this order wrong causes "cannot access before initialization" errors.

### 10.8 Known Limitations

Any rebuild should also incorporate the methodology critiques from Section 8, or explicitly document that they persist. In particular:

- Independent annual sampling in Monte Carlo
- No within-scenario variance
- No correlation modeling
- No passive benchmark comparison

Fixing these would improve model accuracy but adds substantial complexity.
